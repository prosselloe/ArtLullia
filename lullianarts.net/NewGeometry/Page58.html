2.3.5 Quantity
Questions about Quantity
Question 1
Is God better signified by a major quantity of virtue than by a line of major quantity?
Objection
God is virtue, and not a line, and He is infinite. And for this reason, a major quantity of virtue signifies God better than does a line of major quantity.
Solution
Go to the principle of quantity, and you are right with regard to the signification of an intellect stripped of all imaginable species, but you are wrong with regard to imagination and intellect, given that the intellect, when it imagines a major line, considers the infinite extension of divine substance free of any quantity.

Question 2
If there were any quantity in God, would this quantity make Him finite?
Objection
God is an infinite being, and if there were any quantity in God, He would be finite only with regard to this quantity, but He would be infinite inasmuch as His essence is not entirely pervaded by this quantity.
Solution
Go to the second principle, and you are wrong, given that God is a simple, indivisible substance, which He would not be if He were finite due to some natural quantity, while remaining infinite due to another essence free of quantity.

Question 3
Is there one general quantity that gives rise to all other quantities?
Objection
It is obvious that between the quintessence and the four substances of the world, there is a natural division in space, whence it follows that all quantities do not spring from one quantity.
Solution
Go to the third principle, and you are right in saying that there is a division between the quintessence and the four substances of the world, but you are not right with regard to the continuation and succession of the undivided line, and with regard to the prime essence of the world which is one due to one general essence, even though its essence manifests through many species of essences, like wine and oil, which are divided into discrete quantities, but remain under one quantity common to both, and continuous through the quantity of prime matter.

Question 4
Is the whole world entirely full?
Objection
The whole world cannot be full, because if it could be, bodies would be within each other, which is impossible.
Solution
Go to the fourth principle, and you are right in saying that bodies cannot exist within each other, as one body occupies one locus and another body occupies another locus. For instance, a golden cup cannot be in the same locus as a silver cup, while each cup retains its own configuration and shape. But by melting both cups down to make one vase with one shape, the body of the golden cup can be within the body of the silver cup and vice-versa, like one part within another, where both parts compose one quantity.

Question 5
Can the imagination imagine general quantity?
Objection
If the imagination could not imagine general quantity, then it could not imagine specific quantity.
Solution
Go to the fifth principle, and you are right with regard to subalternate general quantity, but wrong with regard to entirely general quantity, like the quantity of heaven and of the continuous lines of its center and diameter, which cannot be sensed.

Question 6
Are all compounds made of simple parts?
Objection
In elemented things, no compound is made of simple parts, for if it were made of simple parts, each part would remain in its own specific simple quantity, and the compound would have no continuous quantity, so that it would be both a compound and not a compound, which is impossible.
Solution
Go to the sixth principle, and you would be right if one simple part could not be within another simple part, for instance, one simple element could not be in another simple element, as it can be through the motion of general mixture, like in the generation of elemented things, and as in gold and silver melded into a coin, as we said earlier.

Question 7
Is arithmetic a subject as proper to the imagination as geometry?
Objection
Because an arithmetician attains numerical quantity more easily than a geometer attains corporeal quantity, the imagination has more virtue in things it attains easily than in those it attains with difficulty.
Solution
Go to the seventh principle, and you are right with regard to the ways in which things are seen and heard, but not with regard to the way things are imagined, given that arithmetic does not have circles and angles as a subject.

Question 8
Does one and the same quantity exist within and outside substance?
Objection
Quantity within substance cannot exist outside substance, nor can external quantity be inside, for if this were possible, it would follow that what is inside is identical to what is outside, which is impossible.
Solution
Go to the eighth principle, and you are right inasmuch as quantity, color and shape are quantities distinct from the inner quantity of substance, but you are wrong inasmuch as these distinct quantities make up one continuous compound quantity which comprehensively measures all extremes of substance.

Question 9
What is the prime principle of the division of substance?
Objection
The prime principle of the division of substance is corruption
Solution
Go to the ninth principle, and you are right with regard to material division, but wrong with regard to formal division, which is made first by dividing form.

Question 10
Is substance divisible before quantity?
Objection
Obviously, substance is divided by the division of quantity, and therefore quantity is divided before substance.
Solution
Go to the tenth principle, and although substance is divided by the division of quantity, it does not mean that division is in quantity before substance, given that quantity cannot be without a subject. And because substance is divided by the division of its parts from one another, quantity is divided by accident, and is consequent to the division of substance.
