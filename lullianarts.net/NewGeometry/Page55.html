2.3.2 Lines
Questions about Lines
Question 1
Since lines are essential to figures, can figures be terminated by lines?
Objection
A figure cannot be terminated and enclosed by a line, because if it could, then an essence could terminate itself, which is impossible.
Solution
Go to the first principle, and you are right in saying that an essence does not terminate itself, but a line, although it is essential to a figure, terminates the figure just as a point terminates the line of which it is a part, and so a line is terminated in itself at some point, just as the whole is terminated in its parts and the parts are terminated in their whole.

Question 2
Does a line have matter?
Objection
A line has no matter, for if it had any matter, it would be a body, and one body would be within another, because a line exists in a figure which has a body.
Solution
Go to the second principle, and in saying that a line has no matter, because if it had any matter it would be a body, you would be right if it had its own matter essentially distinct from the body's matter. But a human shape, for instance, is comprised of lines and human essence, and the man's matter is something common to his shape and its lines.

Question 3
Does a surface have matter?
Objection
A surface has no matter, because if it had any, it would be a body, not an accident.
Solution
Go to the third principle of lines, and in saying that surface has no matter, because if it did, it would be a body, you are mistaken because the contiguousness of lines is an accident that converts with the surface of the body that makes up the substance of the surface consisting of lines made of points which have a corporeal essence and which constitute and compose the body with lines.

Question 4
What is a line full of?
Objection
A line is not full of anything, as it is a first basic ingredient that fills a figure.
Solution
Go to the fourth principle of lines, and you are right in saying that the line is a first basic ingredient that fills a figure, but it could not fill it unless it were itself full of something.

Question 5
Given that a line is long by nature, what gives it its natural width?
Objection
A line is naturally wide when it is full of wide points.
Solution
Go to the fifth principle, and you are right in saying that a wide line is naturally made of wide points, inasmuch as a surface is, by nature, made of wide points.

Question 6
Which line is the fullest?
Objection
Theree is no line is as full as a straight line, given that straightness is more proportioned to a line than obliquity.
Solution
Go to the sixth principle of lines where it is indicated that the more continuous a line is, the fuller it is; and as there is no break or division in a circular line, it is fuller than a straight line; and when you say that straightness is better suited to a line than obliquity, you are right with regard to lines that do not belong to spherical or round bodies which are more mobile than bodies that are not round.

Question 7
Can there be an infinite line?
Objection
God has infinite power, and a line made of points can extend point by point to infinity, and thus God can make, or create one point after another, so that an infinite line can exist by God's power and will.
Solution
Go to the seventh principle of lines, and you are right in saying that God has infinite power. However, because no compound thing made of finite parts can have an infinite nature, even though God with his nature and power could create an infinite line, nevertheless, a line cannot, by nature, be infinite. And just as God cannot cause infinite growth from all eternity, he cannot do this because the subject cannot receive it.

Question 8
With which line does nature express the greatest appetite?
Objection
There is no line as continuous and full as a circular line, and therefore the greatest natural appetite must be expressed by a circular line.
Solution
Go to the eighth principle, and you would be right if power and object could meet as directly through a circular line as through a diametrical line.

Question 9
Does a line occupy space?
Objection
A line occupies no space, given that a line is not a body, and without a body, it cannot occupy space.
Solution
Go to the eighth principle, and you are right in saying that a line is not a body. But inasmuch as a line is made of points, it is a part of a body, and inasmuch as it is a part of a body, one line occupies another line's place, like a black line placed over a white line occupies the place of the other line inasmuch as it does not participate with the white surface.

Question 10
Is the influence shed by heaven on things below disposed in a circular line, or a square line, or a triangular one?
Objection
Because stars have spherical and round bodies, they influence things below with an influence which is round like a circle.
Solution
Go to the tenth principle of lines, and what you say is right. But because the square line stands naturally between the circular line and the triangular line, the influence first comes in a circular figure, then in a square one, and because they incline towards loci proper to their specific natures, they change into a triangular habit, and this was indicated earlier in the figure of the planets.
