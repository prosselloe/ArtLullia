1.3.11 Fig 20 - the elemental spheres
FIGURE OF THE ELEMENTAL SPHERES
This figure is included in this science to provide a doctrine enabling one to imagine the spheres of the 4 elements and the spheres of the 7 planets in accordance with their quantitative, substantial and essential natural values; and this according to their quantitative, essential and specific natural values.
And this is done by following the order of the half triangle and the figure of 3 circles which we already drew, where a contained circle is equivalent to a containing one.  In this figure, a. signifies the sphere of fire, b. the sphere of air, c. the sphere of water, and d. the sphere of earth, and likewise with the other letters according to the regions where they are found, and the figure signifies the entire body that fills all the space within the lunar sphere.
The figure is divided into 4 parts by 4 diametric lines to signify that the world is divided and extended into 4 regions as signified by the figure.
Because fire has more virtue than air, it must have more form and less matter than air, and likewise with air as compared to water, and the same with water and earth, and due to this, d. must be a thick and concave body, and likewise with a.b. And natural order requires that c. be thicker than b., and b. than a., so that d. can be contained by c., and c. by b., and b. by a., and here we can realize that elemental form has more extensive capacity than matter, occupies more space than matter, and has a subtler nature.
As we imagine the layout of this figure, we can also imagine how the planetary spheres are situated, where the lunar sphere is thicker than the sphere of Mercury, and the sphere of Mercury thicker than the sphere of Venus, and so on with the rest of the planets. And this must be true, to ensure natural mutual correspondance in the order in which planets and elements are situated, so that the influences from above be better received in things below.
With this figure, one can imagine how vapors and winds are situated when forming acute and wide angles as they ascend and descend, for instance: angle d. is acute as compared to angle c., and angle c. is acute as compared to angle b., and likewise with a., and this is why vapors expand and dilate as they ascend, and when they descend, they contract and condense so as to take up less space. Therefore, the gross and heavy vapors that descend below, create winds made of subtler and lighter vapors moved by the upper vapors whose imprint they bear, as they have an appetite to descend to things below and occupy places that contain vapors from which winds materially arise that cannot move up and down without moving transversely.
