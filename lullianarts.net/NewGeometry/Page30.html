1.3.19 Fig 29 - the planets
FIGURE OF THE PLANETS
This figure of the planets is included in this art to provide a doctrine for metaphorically measuring the influences that planets have on things here below, and we want to discuss this influence briefly as follows: a. in this figure stands for Saturn, b. for Jupiter, c. for Mars, d. for the Sun, e. for Venus, f. for Mercury and g. for the Moon.
And we say that h. as well as i.k.l.m.n.o. are regions into which the planets instill their virtues. Now, a. is of a cold and dry earthy complexion, and b. is of an airy complexion, and when a. instills its influence straight into h. as when a. is at midheaven, it then instills more virtue than when it instills it laterally. And we have experience of this with a burning mirror by which the Sun transmits more of its heat through acute angles than through obtuse ones. Therefore, as a. directly instills its virtue through an acute angle to its opposite, it produces its influence through a straight line, and when its opposite is oblique, as for instance, if h. were closer to b. than to a., then the influence of a. is proportionally weakened because b. occupies h. and a. does not have as strong an influence through a transversal line than through a perpendicular one.
And the things said about a.b.h. can be said likewise for the remaining lines, according to their conditions. Supposing that region h. is of an earthy complexion, a. can instill more of its virtue into it than if it were of a complexion opposite to earth's. And a. can instill more dryness than cold, because dryness is earth's proper quality, whereas cold is appropriated to earth by water. Given all these things, this figure of the planets is useful for imagining their influences and for mentally measuring major and minor, straight and oblique influences.
