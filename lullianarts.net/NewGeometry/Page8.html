1.1.5 Fig 5 - triangulature of the square

We proved the squaring and the triangulature of the circle; now, following these two proofs, we want to prove the triangulature of the square, where one and the same circle has the same value as one square, and the square has the same value as the circle, and if the same relation of value exists between the circle and the triangle, then the square and the triangle must necessarily be equivalent, because if they were not equivalent, they could not be equivalent to one and the same circle, therefore one triangle and one square are equivalent, and we can sense this, inasmuch as a.b.c. signify that they are worth the surface covered by e.f.g.
To put the square and the triangle together, we must first draw a triangle, then find its center, and make this center coincide with that of the square, and thus the triangle and the square are equivalent in capacity, and they have one and the same center, as they mutually contain each other.
