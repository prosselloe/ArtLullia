1.3.21 Fig 31 - telling time by day
A QUADRANT FOR TELLING TIME BY DAY

In the quadrant there are three circles: one is divided into 90 degrees, another into 24 parts, each part is called a half-hour. Another circle contains 24 lines signifying the hours, for instance, a. signifies the first half hour of daylight as well as the final half-hour at sunset; b. stands for the second half-hour of the morning and the second last half-hour of daylight. And so with the other hours in their own way, and as a. is a rising half-hour and b. is another rising half-hour, a.b. make up one hour in the morning which is the first in the day, and as they descend, a.b. make up another hour which is the last hour of daylight; c.d. make up the second ascending hour of daylight, and the second descending evening hour; e.f. make up the third ascending morning hour and the third descending evening hour; and so on in sequence from the first letter to the last.
Because the sun casts a longer shadow near sunrise and sunset than in the third hour, or at noon, or in the ninth hour, and as it casts longer shadows in the ninth hour than at noon, we must investigate and determine how many of the 90 degrees belong to each daylight hour.
Now the first hour has more degrees than the second because the shadow is longer in the first hour than in the second, and likewise, the second hour has more degrees than the third, because the sun casts a longer shadow in the second hour than in the third, and the eye can see this at sunrise and sunset when the sun casts gigantic shadows of people, and at noon when people's shadows are just the size of people. And the same with the third hour, the fourth, the fifth, and so on.
These degrees must be divided according to the artificial day, and as a day takes 90 degrees in the equatorial region where an artificial day in the month of June has 24 hours, and as the division into hours must be made according to the days at the equator, we make this division as follows:  we give 6½ degrees up the edge of the quadrant to the first hour signified by a.b., and to the final hour of daylight, at sunset, we give another 6½ degrees, so that a.b. have 13 of the 90 degrees shared between 2 hours.
Give 6 ascending degrees to the second daylight hour, and to the second last hour of daylight give another 6 degrees;
11 degrees in ascent and descent to the hours signified by e.f.,
10 degrees in ascent and descent to hour g.h., 
9 degrees in ascent and descent to hour i.k.; 
8 degrees in ascent and descent to hour l.m.,
7 degrees in ascent and descent to hour n.o.,
6 in ascent and descent to hour p.q., 
5 degrees in ascent and descent to hour r.s.,
4 degrees in ascent and descent to hour t.u., we give
3 degrees in ascent and descent to hour w.x.,
2 degrees in ascent and descent to hour y.z..
This division includes all 90 degrees in the 24 hours of the day, which cannot be done by any other division, or else there would have to be more or less than 24. And you can know this by experience, as I did.
