1.3.13 Fig 22 - element mixture
FIGURE OF THE MIXTURE OF ELEMENTS


In this figure, a. stands for fire, b. for air, c. for earth and d. for water. And we attribute the triangle with a. near the center to fire, the triangle with b. near the center to air, the triangle with d. near the center to water, and likewise with earth.
Fire heats air and receives dryness from earth. Therefore, in the triangle of fire, a.b. are in a concordant aspect, as well as a.c., but b.c. are in a contrary aspect, giving rise to 3 measurements of action and passion, each of which is of a dual nature, for as fire instills its own quality of heat into air, it also instills its dryness which it receives from earth.
Therefore, the aspect between a. and b. involves 2 actions and 2 passions, through one of which there is concordance between fire and air, whence generation arises in elemented and generated substances like animals, plants and metals; and through the contrariety between a. and b. due to dryness and moisture, ensues the corruption of old substances and the disposition to generate new substances.
What we said about a.b. also applies to a.c., and the aspect between b. and c. is entirely contrary because it is made of dryness and moisture. Therefore line b.c. consists entirely of contrary qualities and measures.
What we said about triangle a. can also be said about triangles b.c.d. For instance, b. receives heat from a. and gives its moisture to water, and so it  heats water inasmuch as air entering into water carries heated moisture with it. Thus, as air enters into water, water receives 3 qualities, namely moisture, heat and dryness. And likewise, all the elements enter into one another through their proper and appropriated qualities, while no element ever abandons its own quality, which it cannot relinquish because it is its own subject.
Following what we have signified with a.b.c.d., you can, in your imagination, know and measure the quantities of action, passion, influx and reflux of the elements in compounds through generation and corruption. You can also know how the simple elements enter into one another through composition while each simple element retains its own essential nature even as it exists in composition throughout the said mixture, as in a coin made of gold, silver, copper and tin in which all 4 metals are compounded, melded and merged into each other as parts in parts and as parts of the whole, where this whole is the coin, while each metal retains its own essence and natural being as the subject of its own quality and natural virtue.
This figure is useful for physicians to know, as well as for natural scientists and geometers, inasmuch as physicians can procure health following the conditions proper to the elements and the way they enter into mixture; and natural scientists can fathom the secrets of nature. Also, geometers can use it to imagine the behavior of simple and compound points, and then go on to the lines, situations and figures displayed by substance.
