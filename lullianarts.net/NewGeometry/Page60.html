2.3.7 Capacity
Questions about Capacity
Question 1
What does capacity consist of ?
Objection
Capacity is due to the disposition of matter in a body which is disposed to contain something.
Solution
Go to the first principle of capacity with its corollary, and you are right with regard to the substantial act. But with regard to the natural spaces signified by the principle of capacity and formally resulting from it, given that squares and circles exist in nature, substances or figures have the capacity to contain angles and circles, due to which one body can be contained in another, and one part in another, to make up one composition, and linear continuity in substance.

Question 2
Do surface and concavity equally constitute capacity?
Objection
If surface and concavity were equally meant for one purpose, it would follow that the supreme surface of heaven would have the capacity to contain some kind of body within its concave space, and so on ad infinitum, which is impossible.
Solution
Go to the second principle, and you are right with regard to the supreme surface, but this question asks about surfaces covering concavity in bodies, like the sphere of fire whose concavity's purpose is to contain the sphere of air.

Question 3
Does a very tiny point have the capacity to contain another point?
Objection
A point so small that it cannot be any smaller has no capacity to contain another point which is not that small.
Solution
Go to the third principle, and you would be right, if every point existed actually and fully as something on its own. But you are wrong, inasmuch as points can contain one another due to natural concavity, generation and fusion.

Question 4
In things below heaven, is there any point that is not concave?
Objection
By experience, we see that a grain of wheat, or a grain of sand is full, and not concave.
Solution
Go to the fourth principle, and you are right with regard to the fullness of dense bodies, but you are wrong with regard to the natural capacity and concavity that exists in a grain of wheat or sand, as each contains many parts, some of which contain other parts that go into the composition of a grain of wheat.

Question 5
Does capacity exist as much on account of long points as on account of wide or round points?
Objection
Capacity and concavity are more suited to long points than to other points, since acute angles are made of long points and have greater concavity with their longer lines.
Solution
Go to the fifth principle which indicates that capacity is due more to width than to length, and to the circle more than to the angle. And you are wrong, even though long lines are more continuous through acute angles than through right angles, given that a right angle contains more in its width than does an acute angle in its narrowness.

Question 6
Does any line have the capacity to be divided into points equal to itself?
Objection
No line contains any actual points, as it is constituted of points, for if it had any, it could be neither continuous nor full, because the configuration and situation of the points would impede its perfect continuity. Therefore, a line cannot be divided into points that are actually in it, but into points that are in it potentially.
Solution
Go to the sixth principle, and you would be right if in the line some points were not within other points. And they actually exist within each other just like gold and silver melded in a coin. But you are wrong in saying that the constituting points of a line are in potentiality in the line, for if they were, the line would be made of non actual points, so that it would have nothing for constituting its act, but it would rather have to remain as something potential.

Question 7
Is time made of parts?
Objection
Time must consist of parts, because if it did not consist of parts, nothing could move in time, nor would there be any past or future time.
Solution
Go to the seventh principle, and you are wrong in saying that time must be made of parts so that things can move in time from the present to the past and also the future, given that things move in time as they exist in time like a sailor lying or sitting in a boat, and thus, time moves through the parts of days and hours from one part to another, and by reason of this motion, past and future time arises.

Question 8
Why does the circle have greater containing capacity than the square and the triangle?
Objection
What gives the circle greater containing capacity than the square and triangle, is that it has more concave points than the square or the triangle.
Solution
Go to the eighth principle, and you are right in saying that the circle has more concave points than do the square or triangle if it contains this square or triangle, but if the figures are all mutually equivalent in containing capacity as shown in the plenary figure, each figure has as many concave points as the others.

Question 9
Since wine is a full body, and water is a full body, how can both have the capacity to contain the other in a cup in which they are mixed?
Objection
Wine and water are elemented substances composed of points and lines, and in the cup, one body is close to the other so that the points and lines of both bodies are also close.
Solution
Go to the ninth principle, and you are wrong in speaking of the mixture of wine and water in terms of closeness, because if this closeness did not bring them within each other, neither would the body of the wine and water be full, nor would it have linear continuity.

Question 10
Can one quantity be composed of many simple quantities?
Objection
Quantity is an accident, and an accident cannot be composed of accidents, since in every composition, there must be matter that cannot have any accidents.
Solution
Go to the tenth principle, and you are right in saying that composition does not simply arise from accidents, but because the substances of the accidents enter into composition, their quantities, qualities and other accidents make up common quantities, qualities and so forth, as for instance in the taste of a rose and the taste of sugar that result in one taste, and likewise with other such things.
