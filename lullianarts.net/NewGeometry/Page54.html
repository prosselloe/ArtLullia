2.3.1 Points
Questions about Points
Question 1
Is the point the first most basic ingredient of composition, or is it the line?
Objection
If the point, and not the line, were the most basic ingredient of composition, it would follow that a line is composed of discrete points and cannot be continuous or full.
Solution
Go to the first principle of points with its corollary, where the answer is signified by saying that a point is part of a line which mediates between the point and the subject composed of lines. Hence, the point must be the first simple ingredient of composition, or else, composition would not be composed of prime simple ingredients, which is impossible. And the objection that if the point were the first simple ingredient of composition, then the line would be made of discrete points and could not be full, is not true, given that in a line, every point is in every other point just as each simple element is in the others, and composition results from their mixture, as fire enters into air and gives it its heat, fire's heat does not abandon its own subject, but rather enters with it, and likewise with the other elements, so that in mixture, the line is continuous throughout the length, width and depth of the body.

Question 2
In nature, is there a common point?
Objection
In nature, there cannot be any common point, because if there were such a point, it would be made of parts, and mixture would be both common and not common, given that all common things are common due to their parts.
Solution
Go to the second principle of points and its corollary where the answer to the question is signified. Now your objection would be true if all common points were common due to the compound parts of a common point; but a simple and common point can be common although it has no parts or quantitatively distinct parts in lines, loci and figures, like the simple form of fire inasmuch as it is one indivisible point common to all compound and secondary forms of fire, and to their matter.

Question 3
Are surfaces made of points, or lines?
Objection
No surface can be made of points, given that a point has no length, width or depth. And as there cannot be any surface without length, width and depth, it follows that surfaces are made of lines.
Solution
Go to the third principle of points, and when you say that surfaces cannot consist of points, you are merely referring to simple points, given that simple points have no length, width and depth. But this is not true of compound points, given that all compound points have length, width and depth.

Question 4
Does corporeal nature contain indivisible points?
Objection
In corporeal nature which is continuous throughout all of its subjects, there is no vacuum. But there would be a vacuum if corporeal nature contained some indivisible point of its own essence, because such a point could not be divided into a quantity of discrete parts.
Solution
Refer to the fourth principle, where the solution is indicated; and you would be right if the point you mentioned had length, width and depth; but division cannot move this kind of point to generation and corruption in elemental nature.

Question 5
Can a point that is the subject of an angle be divided?
Objection
A point that is the subject of an angle cannot be divided, because if it could be divided, it would follow that the angle would have no subject and no matter in which to exist.
Solution
Look up the corollary to the fifth principle, and you would be right if the point of the angle were not a body and a compound, but it is a body, and as such, it is a compound subject of an angle, so it can be divided into its components.

Question 6
Could some sensible point be indivisible?
Objection
A point made with a needle, and a very tiny colored point can be sensed, but remain indivisible because they have no long, wide and deep lines.
Solution
Go to the corollary of the sixth principle; and because any point made with a needle is full of air which is compound and divisible, and because a colored point is a compound body with a compound surface, your statement is not true.

Question 7
Is there a general point in nature?
Objection
If there were a general point in nature, it would have to have specific points, and the specific points would have to have individual points, which would mean that the general point and its species would have to be real, but since genus and species are not real but only intentional things, there can be no general point in nature.
Solution
Go to the seventh principle, and you are wrong in saying that no general point can really exist in nature, because prime matter, which is a point general to all specific points, is a real thing, as are natural goodness and other natural principles like one color, or one whiteness. So likewise, the point at the center is general to the points in the angles, and a point in a right angle is more general than a point in an acute angle, and every one of these generalities is a real thing.

Question 8
In nature, is there really any point so simple that it has nothing else in itself but its own pure essence?
Objection
There can be no simple point in nature, because in any point there must be several things, such as locus, quantity, fullness etc.
Solution
Go to the eighth principle, and when you say that there must be several essences in any point, you are right, given that many essences go into composing a point; but considered as a simple essence inasmuch as it is what it is and not some other essence, there can be single, simple points in nature, like one color, one corporeity etc.

Question 9
In the supreme surface of heaven, is there some point that is not common?
Objection
Any point at the surface of a line must be common, because if it were not common, it could not terminate a line; and because the surface of heaven is made of points, and as it is the terminus of the supreme line, this surface cannot have any point that is not common.
Solution
Go to the ninth principle, and when you say that no point can terminate a line unless it is common, you are right with regard to the nature of the points under the supreme surface of heaven. But because heaven must be terminated, since a body cannot be infinite, the points making up the supreme surface of heaven must be of a nature not shared by the points inside, or else it would be possible for heaven, or some other body to be infinite inasmuch as there would be an infinite process where each point participates with another one above in an endless succession of points, which is impossible. Therefore the topmost points must be of a nature not shared by the points below, and due to this nature, their commonness must finish at the top, so that heaven can have a final termination.

Question 10
Can a point as such move from place to place?
Objection
No point as such can move from place to place, given that a point cannot move without a line.
Solution
Go to the tenth principle, and you are right inasmuch as no point can move without a line just as form cannot move without matter; but you are wrong inasmuch as a point can be moved from place to place by a line just like a sailor is moved from place to place by the motion of a boat.
